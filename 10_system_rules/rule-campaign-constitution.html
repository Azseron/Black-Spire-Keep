RULE - Campaign Constitution

Core Rules Manifest (Canonical)

This document defines the non-negotiable mechanical foundations of the campaign.
If a rule is not stated here, it is either:

  • An optional expansion
  • A GM ruling
  • A derived consequence of these rules

When in doubt, this document overrides all others.

1. System Baseline

The campaign uses GURPS 4th Edition as its mechanical base.

The following books are considered rules-valid sources:

  • GURPS Basic Set (Characters & Campaigns)
  • GURPS Magic
  • GURPS Thaumatology
  • GURPS High-Tech
  • GURPS Dungeon Fantasy (select material)
  • GURPS Monster Hunters (select material)

Rules from these books apply only where not superseded by this manifest.

2. Tone and Power Assumptions

  • Characters are cinematic but mortal
  • Combat is fast, decisive, and dangerous
  • Magic is powerful, flexible, and risky
  • The game assumes competent protagonists, not average civilians

Optional cinematic rules may be used at the GM’s discretion, but core mechanics remain grounded in GURPS resolution.

3. Action Economy

  • Characters may Move and Attack in the same turn without penalty, unless performing:
    • All-Out Attacks
    • All-Out Defenses
  • This rule applies equally to PCs and NPCs
  • Retreats, defenses, and active reactions function normally

This replaces standard GURPS movement/attack limitations.

4. Skill Resolution

All rolls use standard 3d6 roll-under mechanics.

  • Margin of Success (MoS) is always calculated
  • Margin of Failure (MoF) matters when relevant
  • When multiple skills are rolled for a single action, the lowest MoS is used

Critical successes and failures follow Basic Set rules unless explicitly overridden.

5. Unified Symbol Magic System (Core Magic)

Magic does not use fixed spell lists.

All magic is constructed using Symbols, divided into:

Verbs (What magic does)

Create, Move, Control, Sense, Heal, Protect, Weaken

Nouns (What magic affects)

Air, Water, Fire, Earth, Body, Mind, Light, Sound, Plant, Animal, Necromancy, Knowledge, Meta-Magic

A spell consists of:

  • 1 Verb
  • 1–3 Nouns

Example:

  • Create + Fire → Fireball
  • Control + Light → Invisibility

6. Casting a Spell

To cast a spell:

  1. Roll each involved Symbol skill
  2. Determine MoS for each roll
  3. The lowest MoS becomes the spell’s final MoS
  4. MoS determines the spell’s strength and scaling

If any required roll fails, the spell fails.

7. Margin of Success Scaling (Magic)

MoS determines the tier of effect, not just success.

MoS Effect Tier
0–2 Basic effect
3–5 Strong effect
6–8 Advanced / Area
9–11 Elite
12+ S-Rank / Legendary

Exact mechanical outcomes are defined by the GM using these tiers as guidance.

8. Magery and Limits

  • Magery governs:
    • Maximum complexity of spells
    • Maximum MoS spent per effect category
  • A character may not spend more MoS on a single effect category than their Magery level
  • Excess MoS may still be used, but increases magical risk (see Threshold Magic)

9. Threshold-Limited Magic (Energy System)

Magic uses Threshold Magic, not fatigue-based costs.

  • Each spell adds Threshold (T) instead of FP cost
  • Threshold recovers over time at a fixed rate
  • Exceeding Threshold risks Calamities

Typical Threshold Costs

  • Minor spells: 1–3 T
  • Moderate spells: 4–6 T
  • Major / Ultimate spells: 7–10+ T

Calamities are magical, narrative, and potentially severe.

10. Maintaining Spells

  • Maintaining a spell is passive
  • Casting a new spell while maintaining one imposes a –2 penalty per maintained spell
  • Failure affects only the newly cast spell unless Threshold backlash occurs

11. Combat Damage and Effects

  • Damage uses standard GURPS damage types and DR
  • Area effects, homing effects, control effects, etc. are determined by MoS allocation
  • Magic may create effects that mimic Advantages, Attacks, or Conditions, but does not grant permanent traits unless explicitly allowed

12. Equipment and Technology

  • Technology Level is modern (TL8 baseline unless stated otherwise)
  • Equipment legality, black markets, and scarcity are enforced
  • Modern weapons are lethal; armor matters
  • Technomagical items exist but are rare and controlled

13. Monsters and Non-Humans

  • Monsters are ecological entities, not XP bundles
  • Stat blocks emphasize:
    • Behavior
    • Ecology
    • Tactics
  • Monsters may retreat, negotiate, or act irrationally
  • Not all encounters are balanced or fair

14. Advancement

  • Character advancement is point-based
  • Advancement may occur through:
    • Training
    • Study
    • Story milestones
    • Exceptional play
  • Power growth is intentional but controlled

15. GM Authority Clause

The GM:

  • Interprets MoS outcomes
  • Defines narrative consequences
  • Adjudicates edge cases

However, the GM does not override core mechanics without explicitly declaring a house rule.

End of Core Rules

Short answer first, because clarity beats ceremony:

No — not at first.
The Core Rules Manifest should remain one single note.

Now the longer, more interesting answer: why that’s true, and when it stops being true.

Think of the Manifest as a Constitution

A constitution is powerful precisely because it’s hard to fragment.

Your Core Rules Manifest is not a reference manual. It’s a statement of reality. The moment you split it into multiple notes, three bad things start happening:

  1. Authority diffuses
    Players (and future-you) start asking “Which note overrides which?”
  2. Drift sneaks in
    One section gets quietly updated, another doesn’t. Congratulations — you’ve invented accidental edition wars.
  3. Obsidian starts lying
    Links make everything look equally important, even when it isn’t.

A constitution lives or dies by coherence. One file forces coherence.

The Correct Mental Model

Right now, your manifest should be:

One document that can be printed, handed to a player, and sworn upon.

If you cannot print it as a single artifact without losing meaning, it’s too fragmented.

When Sections Should Become Their Own Notes

There is a correct time to split — just not yet.

You split only when a section gains subordinate structure.

Here’s the rule of thumb:

A section becomes its own note only when it needs children.

Let’s apply that to your examples.

❌ Do NOT Split Yet

1. System Baseline
This is declarative. It names books and authority. It has no moving parts.

If it were its own note, you’d gain nothing and risk someone “editing the foundation.”

Same for:

  • Tone and Power Assumptions
  • Action Economy
  • GM Authority Clause

These are axioms, not systems.

⚠️ Candidate for Future Splitting (But Not Now)

Unified Symbol Magic System

Right now, it belongs in the manifest as a law.
Later, it will absolutely spawn:

  • Magic Allocation Tables
  • Example Spells
  • Edge Cases
  • Design Rationale
  • Player vs GM guidance

When that happens, the manifest keeps the summary, and a separate document becomes:

“Unified Symbol Magic — Full Rules”

The manifest links outward, but never imports detail inward.

✅ Definitely Split Later

These will eventually want their own notes:

  • Monsters (already happening naturally)
  • Equipment economies
  • Advancement rules
  • Faction mechanics
  • Dungeon / Gate rules

But note the pattern:
Those are procedural systems, not constitutional laws.

The Obsidian Rule That Saves Sanity

Here’s the rule that prevents vault collapse:

Top-level documents never link sideways. They only link downward.

Your Core Rules Manifest should:

  • Sit at the root
  • Have no backlinks that alter it
  • Be referenced, not edited casually

Everything else orbits it.

A Practical Obsidian Structure (Minimal, Clean)

For now, I’d suggest:

00 - CORE RULES MANIFEST.md 01 - Magic System (Expansion).md 02 - Combat & Damage Clarifications.md 03 - Monsters & Ecology.md 04 - Equipment & Economy.md GM - Design Notes (Private).md

Notice:

  • The manifest stays singular
  • Expansions explain, but do not redefine
  • GM notes are quarantined

Final Thought (This Is the Important Part)

Fragmentation feels productive.
Coherence is productive.

You’ve already done the hard work: designing a system that hangs together. The manifest’s job is to freeze that coherence.

Once the ice is solid, then you can build cities on top of it.

Next time, if you want, we can:

  • Mark which sentences in the manifest are link anchors
  • Decide which sections are immutable vs versioned
  • Or design a “Player Cut” vs “GM Cut” without duplicating rules

You’re building this the right way.
